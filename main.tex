\section{Background}

\subsection{Discrete Fourier Transform}
The Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) is a fundamental tool used in digital signal processing that 
enables sophisticated analysis and synthesis of discrete-time signals \cite{lyons2010understanding}.   
The DFT transforms a signal from the time domain into its frequency-domain representation,  
allowing the signal to be expressed as a sum of sinusoids \cite{lyons2010understanding}. The DFT accepts a finite 
sequence of $N$ discrete samples and produces $N$ complex-valued outputs, each representing the signal's magnitude 
and phase at one of $N$ evenly-spaced frequency bins. 
The output of the DFT can be better understood as a sequence of $N$ complex numbers representing amplitude and phase for each analysis frequency \cite{mcfee_dstbook}.  
Mathematically, the DFT is defined as: 
\begin{align}
  X[m] = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n] \, e^{-j 2\pi n\frac{m}{N}}
\end{align}
where $N$ is the number of samples from the input signal $x[n]$, $n$ denotes the time-domain index, $m$ represents the frequency bin index, 
$n$ is the index of the input sequence in the time-domain and $j = \sqrt{-1}$. Each coefficient in $X[m]$ is complex-valued and can be 
expressed in terms of magnitude and phase, which describe the contribution of a corresponding frequency component to the overall signal \cite{mcfee_dstbook}.
Another important property of the DFT is that it is invertible; meaning a signal can be transformed from the frequency domain to the time domain. It can be described
mathematically as: 
\begin{align}
  x[n] = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} X[m] \, e^{j 2\pi n\frac{m}{N}}
\end{align}
Since the forward direction of the DTF breaks a signal into its fundamental sinusoids, the inverse DFT perfoms the opposite.
It computes each component of a signal $x[n]$ as the average of the $n$th samples of the DFT's fundamental sinusoids \cite{mcfee_dstbook}.
The inverse DFT is a crucial component of the phase vocoder as it allows for a signal to be transformed back to the time domain after being modified
in the frequency domain.In practice, the DFT is much too slow for modern applications. Therefore, applications today use the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT), 
an optimized algorithm for computing the DFT. A common method is the Cooley and Tukey algorithm, which requires that the input signal length be a power of two \cite{mcfee_dstbook}. 

\subsection{Windowing}
Windowing is a function used to isolate parts of a discrete time signal so that analysis on the signal can be performed on contiguous blocks \cite{barry2008time}.
More specifically, the input signal is multiplied by a windowing function whose domain is nonzero for a particular range. This results in a small localized snapshot of the input signal
where every value outside of the window's domain is reduced to zero \cite{royer2019pitch}. Figure 1 below demonstrates the functionality of a window function.

\subsection{Short-Time Fourier Transform}
The Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) is a time-frequency representation used to compute the DFT of contiguous windowed segments of a signal. In practice, 
however, the STFT uses the FFT as this is more computationally efficient.
This is accomplished by multiplying the signal by a windowing function so that only a small portion of the signal is nonzero \cite{dolson1986phase}. 
Mathematically, it is similar to equation $(1)$ but with a few modifications: the addition of a windowing function, the domain of the input signal, 
and how the output is interpreted. Below is the STFT: 
\begin{align}
  X[m,k] = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n+kH]w[n]e^{-2\pi jmn/N}
\end{align}
Where $X[m, k]$ is the 2-dimensional output array indexed by both frequency bin and time frame and $x[n + kH]$ is the section of the input signal 
determined by frame index and hop size \cite{royer2019pitch}. The outcome of the STFT is a representation that transforms a signal from the time 
domain to the frequency domain. In this form, the signal's frequency components can be systematically modified and recombined to reconstruct a new signal. 


Since the STFT only computes the DFT of a particular section of a given signal, it must be shifted in order to process another section of the signal. 
The amount in which the STFT is shifted is known as the \textit{Hop Size}, denoted as $H$. The hop size is determined by how much the successive STFTs should overlap, 
more on this in Section 4.3. Typical hop sizes include $75\%$, $50\%$, $33.3\%$, and $20\%$ overlap \cite{degotzen2000traditional}.  
Lastly, just as the DFT has an inverse, so to does the STFT. However, instead of reconstructing the entire signal, the inverse STFT only reconstructs a particular
chunk of a signal. This is especially useful in reconstructing a signal after modifying it in the frequency domain. 


\section{Related Works}

\subsection{Classical Phase Vocoder Approaches}
Pitch-shifting has been approached by a number of time--frequency methods. 
Early methods, such as Dolson's (1986), employed the use of the Short-Time 
Fourier Transform in conjunction with the phase vocoder algorithm. 
In particular, Dolson describes a pitch-shifting method that relies on 
time-scale manipulation to modify the pitch of a given signal \cite{dolson1986phase}. 
For example, by stretching a signal, making it longer in the time domain, 
and then rescaling it back to its original length, the overall frequency 
content of the signal is effectively decreased, which corresponds to 
lowering the signal's pitch. Dolson acknowledges, however, that this 
approach is imperfect: specific constraints must be enforced when 
performing a pitch shift, as failing to do so may introduce unwanted 
artifacts into the signal or result in a reconstructed signal that has 
lost information \cite{dolson1986phase}. While effective for monophonic (single note) pitch scaling, 
this classical time-scale modification framework treats spectral bins independently, 
which can lead to phase incoherence and unwanted artifacts. 
These limitations motivated subsequent refinements to the phase vocoder algorithm.

\subsection{Phase Vocoder Optimizations}

Unlike the original process chain described by Dolson (1986), 
which applies a uniform scaling factor to all spectral components, 
later work by Laroche and Dolson (1999) introduces refinements that improve 
frequency coherence \cite{laroche1999new}. In particular, they highlight a new 
peak-detection paradigm, where peaks refer to how much a particular frequency 
is in the input signal. Once the peaks are detected, they can be shifted around 
by a pitch-factor and unlike the version described by Dolson (1986), the pitch-factor 
is not restricted to an integer amount \cite{laroche1999new}.
 
In addition to optimizing frequency resolution, modern implementations of the phase vocoder 
shift away from the standard two-step timescale and resample approach and 
adopt an analysis and resynthesis paradigm \cite{degotzen2000traditional}. 
The new approach leverages phase information obtained from the STFT step to more accurately 
reconstruct the modified signal \cite{ degotzen2000traditional}. More specifically, a phase adjustment 
value can be derived from the frequency bins across two frames and when added to the original frequency 
approximation the accuracy of the frequency estimation is increased \cite{lim2007phasevocoder}.


\subsection{Alternative Pitch-Shifting Methods}

The prior sections highlight the phase vocoder approach to pitch-shifting, a frequency-domain algorithm that 
converts a signal from the time domain to the frequency domain to compute the pitch shift. An alternative approach uses time-scale modification (TSM) algorithms,
 which operate entirely in the time domain \cite{rai2019analysis}. 
 A common algorithm belonging to the TSM family is the Overlap-Add (OLA) algorithm \cite{royer2019pitch}.

The idea behind these algorithms is that pitch shifting and time scaling are essentially the same; meaning if you have one, 
either a pitch shifter or time scaler, then you effectively have the other. OLA makes use of this paradigm. The algorithm first partitions the input signal into frames, much like the STFT. 
Then, if the goal is to pitch down the signal, when resynthesizing the signal it is constructed in such a way that extra space is added between the reconstructed frames, increasing the length of the signal. 
On the contrary, if the goal is to make the signal's pitch higher, then the algorithm reconstructs the signal in such a way where the space between contiguous frames is reduced, effectively shortening the signal's length \cite{royer2019pitch}.

Since TSM algorithms operate in the time domain and thus change the input signal's duration, either lengthening or shortening it, these algorithms must incorporate a resample step in order to convert the modified signal back to the original signal's duration \cite{rai2019analysis}.
Thus, these alternative algorithms are viable options; however, purely TSM-based algorithms are often outperformed by frequency-based algorithms \cite{royer2019pitch}.

The phase vocoder is a name given to a group of algorithms designed to process an input signal in the frequency domain \cite{reiss2014audioeffects}. This paper specifically focuses on the application of pitch shifting, the process of either increasing or decreasing an audio signal's pitch. 
In particular, this work utilizes a phase vocoder-based pitch shifting algorithm, as it is a computationally efficient method for analyzing and modifying an input audio signal, and is the backbone of many real-time pitch shifting audio plugins used today \cite{royer2019pitch}.


The following subsections will explain the inner workings of the phase vocoder algorithm and its application to pitch shifting. 
The first subsection will explain the analysis step and how the STFT breaks an audio signal into frames. 
The second subsection will cover the modification step and explain how pitch shifting is computed in the frequency domain. 
Finally, the last subsection will explain how the modified signal is synthesized back into an output signal.


\subsection{Analysis}
The first step of the phase vocoder algorithm is the analysis step. The primary goal of this step is to break an input audio signal 
into a succession of overlapping frames so that each frame is processed individually. The analysis step segments the signal into frames
because it is designed to operate in a real-time environment, such as an audio plugin. In this setting, the phase vocoder does not have access to 
the entire signal at once, as it is being continuously fed into the phase vocoder from a source. Thus, the phase vocoder is not concerned with the global scope
of the signal, it only needs to process the signal locally.  


In practice, the STFT is used to partition the audio signal into frames of length $N$.
This is accomplished by multiplying the signal frame by the Hanning window function. Once the signal frame has been isolated, 
the FFT is computed on the windowed signal chunk, converting it from the time domain to the frequency domain. To process the next frame, 
the analysis window moves ahead by $H$ (hop size) number of samples.

The output of the analysis step is a collection of $M$ frequency bins (see Section 2.3), where each bin contains a complex value representing
the magnitude and phase information for a particular frequency \cite{reiss2014audioeffects}. At this point, the signal frame can be modified in the 
frequency domain, such as altering the pitch.

\subsection{Modification}
% In this section I will explain how the phase vocoder is used the phase information obtained from the DFT to perform a more accurate
% frequency transformation. Additionally, the phase information is used to better reconstruct the signal in the resynthesis step.
In this section, I will explain how the phase vocoder is used to modify a given input audio signal. After the modification step, 
the audio signal will have been successfully pitch shifted in the frequency domain. 

With the completion of the analysis step, the 
audio signal has been transformed into the frequency domain. The signal is now represented as linearly spaced frequency bins that 
range from 0 to $N/2 + 1$. Each bin $k$ is a complex number of the form $a + jb$ that represents a specific analysis frequency \cite{mcfee_dstbook}. 
An analysis frequency can be thought of as sinusoid that completes an integer number of cycles over an $N$ sample window. Thus, each frequency bin $k$ 
measures the contribution of a sinusoid at frequency $\frac{k}{N} * f_s$, where $f_s$ is the sampling rate \cite{mcfee_dstbook}. 

One shortcoming of the FFT however, is that it is possible for signals frequencies to fall between frequency bins. For example, a typical sampling rate might be $44100$Hz, 
and let our window size be $1024$ samples. To calculate the spacing between frequences we compute: 
\begin{align*}
  \Delta f = \frac{f_s}{N} = \frac{44100}{1024} \approx 43 \text{Hz}
\end{align*} 
Therefore, at bin 0 the analysis frequency is 0Hz, at bin 1 its 43Hz at bin 2 its 86Hz and so on. If a signal's frequency falls 
between one of these bins, its energy is distributed accross all other bins and the actual frequency becomes obscured \cite{lim2007phasevocoder}.
The energy distribution manifests itself as spectral leakage and as seen in Section 2.2 a windowing function helps to reduce the leakage. 

\subsubsection{Computing a More Accurate Frequency}
To more accurately calculate a  signal frame's frequency, the phase vocoder employs the use of the frame's phase information from the FFT.
The core principle behind this computation is that frequency is the derivative of phase. Thus, by using a signal's phase from two different hops
the exact frequency of the signal can be calculated. The equation below mathematically demonstrates the frequency of a signal $\omega$ written as a
first order derivative in terms of phase $\phi$: 
\begin{align}
  \omega[n] = \frac{\phi[n] - \phi[n-H]}{H}.
\end{align} 
Now, we can rearrage Equation 5 to isolate the phase difference:
\begin{align}
  \phi[n] - \phi[n-H] &= \omega[n] H = \frac{2\pi k H}{N}. 
\end{align}
Thus, the phase shift for a sinusoid with a frequency exactly matching a frequency bin over one hop size is given by Equation 7. 
What we now have is a basis to compare the actual signal frequency to. Computing the adjustment factor, also referred to as the phase
remainder $\phi_r$, we can subtract the ideal phase from the actual observed phase \cite{lim2007phasevocoder}: 
\begin{align}
  \phi_r[n] = (\phi[n] - \phi[n-H]) - \frac{2\pi k H}{N}.
\end{align}
In order to keep from getting negative frequenceis, we must wrap $\phi_r$ between $(-\pi, \pi]$ \cite{reiss2014audioeffects}. Once the phase is wrapped, 
we can calculate the the deviation of the observed frequency from the bin frequency: 
\begin{align}
  \omega[n] - \omega_k &= \frac{\text{wrap}(\phi_r[n])}{H}, \text{ and} \\
  \omega[n] &= \frac{\text{wrap}(\phi_r[n])}{H} + \omega_k. 
\end{align}
We now have an equation that computes the exact frequency of the sinusoid. Due to operating in a real-time enviornment however, 
it is more computationaly efficient to compute a fractionl FFT bin number, which represents the frequency in terms of where it would
fall if the bins had infinite resolution. To compute the fractional FFT bin number, we only need to scale Equation 9 by a factor of $2\pi/N$
since $\omega[n] = \frac{2\pi k}{N}$. Doing so yields: 
\begin{align}
  b[n] =  \frac{\text{wrap}(\phi_r[n])N}{2\pi H} + k
\end{align}
and we now have a method to more accurately compute the frequency of our signal.

\subsubsection{Pitch Shifting and Synthesizing a Signal}
Now that we have a more accurate estimation of the frequency of the audio signal, pitch shifting the signal 
is as simple as scaling the frequency $b[n]$ by a pitch shift factor $R$. In doing so, we compute a new fractional
bin $b_s[n]$ where $b_s[n] = Rb[n]$. Finally we calculate the new bin number $k' = \text{floor}(Rk + 0.5)$ 
that will contain the shifted frequency. 

The final part of the modification step is to calculate a new phase value for the shifted frequency. The new phase 
value ensures that the modified signal is reconstruced correctly and does not introduce any discontinuities at the signal
frame's edges \cite{barry2008time}. The calcuation is similar to Equation 6 with a few adjustments. To compute the new phase
value, we are only concerned with how much the phase has changed from one hop to the next, thus we first must compute the phase
remainder of our new frequency: 
\begin{align}
  \phi_{rs}[n] = \frac{2\pi H(b_s[n] - k')}{N}.
\end{align}
Next, we add the $\phi_{rs}$ to the phase of the shifted signal that perfectly fits into a frequency bin to obtain: 
\begin{align}
 \frac{2\pi Hb_s[n] - 2\pi H k'}{N}  + \frac{2\pi k' H}{N} = \frac{2\pi H b_s[n]}{N}.
\end{align}
Recall that $b_s[n]$ is the fractional bin index, hence we have now computed the phase of the actual frequency of the input signal frame. We 
can now add the previous hops phase to the result of Equation 11 to obtain: 
\begin{align}
  \phi_s[n] = \text{wrap}\left(\phi_s[n-H] + \frac{2\pi H b_s[n]}{N}\right)
\end{align}
where $\phi_s[n]$ is the actual phase of this bin at the current hop.  


\subsection{Synthesis}

Once the signal frame has been modified and a new phase value hase been calculated, the IFFT converts the signal back to the time domain. Then the output is reconstructed using
the Constant Overlap-Add (COLA) paradigm. A detailed explanation of COLA can be found here \cite{smith2011sasp}. The key idea is that 
if samples in the window function, offset by $H$, add to a constant $C$, then the window possesses the "Constant Overlap-Add Property" \cite{smith2011sasp}. The 
mathematical expression below demonstrates the COLA property: 
\begin{align}
  \sum_{m=-\infty}^{\infty} w(n - mH) = 1, \quad \forall n \in \mathbb{Z}
\end{align}
and Figure~\ref{fig:cola-property} below graphically shows the COLA Property.

As seen in section 4.3, the phase vocoder uses previous phase values to more accurately estimate a signal's frequency. Thus, by having a larger overlap, 
the phase vocoder more frequently analyzes the input signal which results in more accurate phase calculations \cite{barry2008time}.

\section{Methodology}
The JUCE framework is used to implement the pitch-shifting audio plugin. JUCE is an open-source framework that supports the development of cross-platform audio applications and plugins. 
Its integrated build system allows projects to target multiple plugin formats, such as VST3, AU and more. JUCE projects are written in C++, making it well-suited for developing highly efficient, 
real-time audio plugins. Additionally, JUCE provides a wide range of built-in libraries that support common audio processing tasks, as well as tools for creating graphical user interfaces (GUIs).

Under JUCE's framework, the pitch shifter processes contiguous blocks of an input signal. The signal is passed to the pitch shifter using JUCE's PluginProcessor.ProcessBlock() method. In this method, 
the audio buffer (block of signal) is divided into its respective channels. Then for each channel, its audio samples (discrete data values) are passed to the pitch shifter for futher processing.
Once in the scope of the pitch shifter, each sample in the channel data is stored in an input buffer. Once this buffer has 1024 samples, the buffer will be windowed and the FFT will be computed to 
obtain the signal's magnitude and phase information accrosse frequency bins. To pitch shift the more accurate frequency estimation, a pitch factor is used to scale the frequency components of the signal. 
Once modified the signal is reconstructed by using prior phase information to enforce continuity accross frames, and the inverse STFT is computed to convert the signal back to the time domain. JUCE then takes 
the modified sample data and passes it back to the DAW where it can then be sent to speakers, or additional plugins for further processing.

\section{Conclusion}
This project effectively demonstrates a working implementation of a phase vocoder based ptich shifter. It is able to both pitch up and down an octave (12 semitones), while maintaining 
reasonable audio clarity and introducing minimal artifacts. The solution follows the phase vocoder paradigm: converting the signal from the time domain to the frequency domain, using phase information obtained from 
this step to improve frequency estimations, modifying the signal in the frequency domain, and finally converting the signal back to the time domain.

While this implementation is good at shifting monophonic signals, it struggles at accurately pitch shifting polyphonic signals (multiple notes at once, chords). More advanced pitch shifters
employ techniques such as peak-locking, zero-padding and advanced phase techniques \cite{royer2019pitch}. One notable future improvement would be to examine alternative methods of phase
reconstruction that result in a more complete signal after signal modification.
